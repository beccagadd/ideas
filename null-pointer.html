0x01 · The Claim

"We live in a simulation."

It sounds like an answer. It has the shape of one — a cause, a creator, a reason the universe runs on math. Tech billionaires say it. Podcasts say it. A guy on a dating app says it.

Here's the problem: whatever we're experiencing is real to us, and calling it a simulation achieves nothing. Nothing about your life, physics, or the hard questions changes. The word "simulation" does no work. It's a label wearing an explanation's clothes.

An answer-shaped nothing.
0x02 · The Regress

Ok. Who created the simulation?

Someone in base reality. Ok — what explains their reality? Another simulation? Then who created that one?

The theory doesn't answer the question. It relocates it — one level up, out of reach, forever. That's not an explanation. That's a forwarding address.

0x03 · The Rebrand

It's god with a GPU.

Swap the vocabulary and nothing changes:

Same theology. New casing. And notice the last row: religion at least assigns its creator a motive. Simulation theory can't even do that. It kept the god and deleted the purpose — which makes it the less complete theory of the two. At least faith has the honesty to call itself faith.

0x04 · The Dereference

Follow the claim to its address.

In code, a pointer is an address — it says "the meaning lives over there." A null pointer has the shape of a reference and points at nothing. Follow it and the program crashes.

"We live in a simulation" is a null pointer. It has the shape of an explanation, and when you actually dereference it — who runs it, why, what for — there's nothing at the address.

> dereference("we live in a simulation")
NULL
> dereference(purpose)
NULL
Segmentation fault (explanation not found)
0x05 · The Real Question

What does the machine resolve?

There's one real observation underneath all of it: the universe computes. States transition, lawfully, everywhere, all the time. Fine. But then ask the question everyone skips — what is computation, structurally?

Computation is thermodynamic activity: high-energy states resolving into low-energy states. Tension resolving into structure. Every computation resolves something — that's what makes it computation and not noise.

So if reality is a computation: what does it resolve? What does the "simulation" achieve, structurally? Simulation theorists skip this part entirely, because they don't actually care about the computation — they just want to say we're in a machine. Duh, cool, machine. That's a vibe, not a theory.

Stop coping with vibes. Ask what the machine resolves.
0x06 · The Salvage

Keep the pointer. Free the turtles.

One component of simulation theory is worth stripping for parts before you junk the rest: reference.

A pointer is locally real — actual bits in actual memory — and its meaning lives outside its own address space. That structure is genuinely interesting, and here's the thing: it doesn't need an operator. Nothing about "this points beyond itself" requires anyone at a keyboard.

Our reality can be exactly that: locally real, and pointing outward — embedded in something larger and more fundamental — without a simulator, an overlord, or a rendering farm. Reference without a referee. That's the part that survives. Everything else was turtles.

Locally real. Semantically outward. Nobody at the keyboard.

This page argues one thing and then stops. If the word "simulation" does less for you now, it worked.
