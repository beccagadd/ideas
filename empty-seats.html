An argument about traffic, public transit, and where your time goes.

Stop 1 · The Premise

The car is wrong-sized for its own life.

A car is a ~4,000 lb machine built to move a ~150 lb payload. It carries four to five seats and, on average, about 1.2 people. Then it parks — which is what it does for roughly 95% of its existence.

Payload efficiency~4%
Average occupancy1.2 / 5 seats
Time spent parked~95%

Nobody chose this on purpose. We bought the vehicle for the peak case — the road trip, the airport run, the four passengers we almost never have — and then ran it at the median case forever.

Stop 2 · The X-Ray

Turn on x-ray vision in traffic.

Make every vehicle transparent and leave only the people visible. A packed street is about 5% humans and 95% chairs and armor.

Same street · Vehicles transparent · Bus at 3/4 full

41 cars · 47 people · 117 empty seats  vs  1 bus · 37 people.

This isn't an anti-car morality play. It's a geometry problem. Cars don't tile. Every fix that adds lanes induces more cars, because the shape of the vehicle is the bottleneck, not the width of the road.

Stop 3 · The Bill

Crashes, carbon, and concrete.

Traffic isn't weather. It's geometry — the predictable result of moving people in vehicles that don't tile. And the bill comes due in bodies, dollars, carbon, and land.

US traffic deaths, 2025~37,000
Average cost of owning a car~$12,000 / yr
US emissions from transportation28% — largest sector
Downtown land claimed by roads + parking~half

Thirty-seven thousand deaths a year is a fully-loaded 737 crashing every other day — and it's so normalized we call them "accidents," as if a system that produces the same outcome every year were surprising. Car crashes remain a leading cause of death for young Americans. No other consumer product gets this pass.

Stop 4 · The Real Bill

The real cost, though, is time.

Average hours spent commuting, per worker~230 / yr
Average hours lost to congestion49 / yr
Those hours, at the average wage of $37/hr~$8,650 / yr
A full college semester225 hrs
40 books~240 hrs
Six 40-hour work weeks240 hrs

Fix congestion completely — every lane project succeeds, every light turns green — and the average driver gets back 49 of those 230 hours. The other 180+ are dead by design, because the mode itself demands a machine operator.

Notice: congestion can't be built out of. Add a lane and traffic expands to fill it — induced demand, one of the most replicated findings in transportation research. Every full bus and train is a lane of cars that never showed up.

You cannot pave your way out of a geometry problem.
Stop 5 · The Real Product

A car buys distance. Public transit buys your time back.

A car commute converts an hour of your life into zero usable time. You can't read, can't work, can't fully think — you're operating machinery. The same hour on a decent bus or train belongs to you.

That's full 40-hour work weeks of your life, returned annually.

People compare trip durations and pick the car. They're measuring the wrong variable. A 45-minute drive is 45 minutes lost; a 60-minute ride where you did your reading is free transportation plus a study session.

The correct unit isn't travel time. It's usable time.
Stop 6 · The Dignity Problem

American transit loses on dignity, not economics.

We designed transit as a last resort — the bare minimum for people with no alternative. Design something as a last resort and it gets treated like one. Everyone who can afford to defects, service gets cut, and the spiral continues.

Meanwhile the car quietly costs its owner ten to twelve thousand dollars a year — insurance, fuel, parking, depreciation. That is an enormous budget to compete against, and transit barely tries. You could sell a genuinely luxurious ride — a private nook, a quiet car, a seat that isn't hostile — for a fraction of that and still win, because the physics is on your side: shared rail moves a person for pennies of energy and inches of space.

Design problems are solvable. This is a design problem.
Stop 7 · Proof It Works

Every time someone builds the nice version, people show up.

  • CincinnatiSuburban express buses with coach seats and outlets, running highway-direct to downtown. People who owned cars rode them on purpose — because the ride gave them their morning back.
  • JapanTrains clean and quiet enough that people sleep openly, with premium cars that feel like a lounge. Ridership isn't a mystery there. Dignity is policy.
  • FloridaBrightline built rail with lounges and real seats — and pulls riders who own cars, in the most car-brained state in the country.
  • EuropeOld trains were literally built as private compartments — six seats and a door. The "little independent room" isn't a fantasy. We already had it. We can have it again.

The demand was never missing. The supply was.

Stop 8 · The Ask

Stop building the punishment version.

Fund transit like a product people choose, not a service people endure. Compartments. Wi-Fi. Outlets. Quiet. Cleanliness. Dignity. Charge less than a car costs — which is easy, because almost everything costs less than a car.

Do that, and the x-ray picture fixes itself: fewer empty chairs hauling themselves around, more people getting their hours back.

There is no newsletter. There is no product. There is no course.

This site argues one thing and then stops, like a good bus route. If it changed how you look at traffic, it worked.
